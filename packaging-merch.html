Packaging + Merch

Every bag, cup, and t-shirt is a brand touchpoint. They should all feel like Theory.

The Principle

The bag is the billboard. Treat it like one.

For most customers, the first physical interaction with Theory is a bag of coffee or a to-go cup. That moment carries the full weight of the brand. Clean design, quality materials, zero clutter. The packaging should feel as considered as the roast inside it.

Bag System

Three colorways. One hierarchy.

Dark bags for single origins. Light bags for medium roasts and accessible blends. Copper bags for signature and limited-edition releases. The wordmark is always centered. Origin top-left. Weight top-right. Roast level at the base. No other text on the front face.

If it doesn't look good on a shelf next to the best, redesign it.

Packaging Principle
Product Lines
Single Origins

Direct-sourced beans from specific farms and regions. Dark bag. Origin country, farm name, and tasting notes on the back panel.

House blends with consistent flavor profiles. Copper bag for Collaborative (the flagship). Dark bag for others. Blend name replaces origin.
Steeped Packs
Single Serve
Pre-portioned steep bags for travel and convenience. Individual foil packs in a branded box. Same wordmark hierarchy, scaled down.
Organic Line
Keramos · Qonqona · Peru Decaf
Certified organic offerings. Same bag system with an organic certification mark placed bottom-left on the back panel. Never on the front.

Packaging Guidelines
Do

Use matte finishes on all bags and boxes. The tactile quality reinforces craft. Gloss feels mass-market.

Don't

Add flavor illustrations, bean graphics, or origin maps to the front face. The wordmark is the only visual.

Do

Include a one-line tasting note on the back panel. Keep it specific: "Bright citrus, floral finish." Not "A delightful journey."

Don't

Use more than two colors on any single package. The system is black, white, and copper. Pick two.

The Standard

Would you put this on your counter? Prove it.

The best coffee packaging doubles as shelf decor. If a customer leaves the bag out because it looks good, the design worked. If they hide it in a cabinet, it didn't. That's the test.
